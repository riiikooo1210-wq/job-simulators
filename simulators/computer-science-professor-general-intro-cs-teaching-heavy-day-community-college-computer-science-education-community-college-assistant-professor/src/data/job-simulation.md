# Computer Science Professor Simulation: Intro CS at Mesa Vista Community College

## Job Profile

The player is an assistant professor of computer science at Mesa Vista Community College, teaching a high-contact CSC 101: Introduction to Programming day. The work centers on classroom teaching, office hours, grading, LMS communication, student support, and light department coordination rather than research-lab management. [onet] [yt:cc_teaching_load_notes] [reddit:cc_workload]

Mesa Vista is a fictional public two-year college. Students include recent high-school graduates, working adults, first-generation college students, and career changers, so the professor must explain concepts clearly without assuming a single preparation level. [yt:intro_cs_pedagogy_notes] [reddit:intro_cs_confusion]

## Work Mix

This simulator treats the role as a hybrid classroom-and-desk job. The professor uses Canvas, SpeedGrader, Slack, lesson documents, and LMS announcements, but the work is not purely digital: the professor also teaches in a computer lab, reads the room, coaches students face to face, and turns student confusion into visible evidence for instruction. [onet] [yt:cc_teaching_load_notes] [yt:intro_cs_pedagogy_notes]

The active scenes therefore include digital artifacts, spoken interpersonal work, written deliverables, a support-triage board, and visual classroom reasoning tasks where the player interprets code, predictions, trace evidence, and a quick check. [onet] [reddit:grading_feedback] [reddit:lms_email]

## Day Arc

The morning begins with a Canvas source packet: student messages, a chair request for attendance flags, tutoring-center capacity, a 60-second CS mini cheat sheet, and a Lab 4 pattern showing confusion about `range(5)` and accumulator updates. The player must decide what to handle before the 9:30 class, then draft a short mini-lesson that uses concrete student evidence and the visible reference facts rather than simply repeating the rule. [onet] [yt:cc_teaching_load_notes] [reddit:lms_email]

Before class, the player prepares a short loop reset for the computer lab. Once class starts, the exact opening prediction question, trace-table follow-up, and move-on decision happen live: the player reads their prompt to students, responds to student answers, guides the trace table, and decides aloud whether students are ready for accumulators. Beginner reference cards provide the necessary CS facts so the assessed work stays centered on teaching judgment, not hidden programming knowledge. [yt:intro_cs_pedagogy_notes] [reddit:intro_cs_confusion]

After class, Jayden comes to in-person office hours and says, "I think I am just not a coding person." The player has a short coaching window to ask diagnostic questions, explain one misconception in plain language, preserve confidence without lowering standards, and leave Jayden ready to try a concrete trace-table plan again. [onet] [yt:advising_grading_notes] [reddit:intro_cs_confusion]

By early afternoon, several student-support issues compete for attention: an accommodation letter, repeated missing labs, a broken laptop, an academic-integrity concern, transfer advising, and a class-wide due-date confusion. The player sorts every case using urgency, learning impact, access, equity, policy, and privacy criteria. [onet] [yt:advising_grading_notes] [reddit:cc_workload]

The day closes with written deliverables: a concise FERPA-aware Slack update for the department chair and tutoring center, feedback on two flawed programming submissions, and a Canvas announcement clarifying the next lab without sounding punitive. The grading feedback scene supplies instructor notes and a beginner quick guide, so the player is assessed on translating visible diagnoses into respectful student-facing comments. These artifacts matter because students and colleagues often act on the written record more than on comments made once in class. [onet] [reddit:grading_feedback] [reddit:lms_email]

## Learning Goals

Students using the simulator should feel the pressure of teaching while the room is moving: diagnose confusion, slow down without losing standards, explain code in plain language, and make the next action visible. [onet] [yt:intro_cs_pedagogy_notes]

They should also feel that a community-college professor's work is relational and administrative as well as intellectual: office hours, advising, LMS records, grading language, and department coordination all shape whether students persist. [onet] [yt:advising_grading_notes] [reddit:lms_email]

## Required Work Products

The simulation requires the player to produce a revised mini-lesson plan, run a classroom loop reset through visual reasoning tasks, speak through an in-person office-hour conversation, prioritize student-support cases, answer a department Slack thread, write diagnostic feedback on visible code submissions, and post a Canvas announcement. This keeps the activity centered on real teaching products and interactions rather than trivia about academia. [onet] [reddit:grading_feedback] [reddit:lms_email]

## Rubric Evidence

The final grading uses evidence from the player's first-move decision, mini-lesson draft, classroom prompt choice, live trace-table discussion transcript, office-hour transcript, support triage board and rationale, Slack update, grading feedback, and LMS announcement. Rubric judgments should reward players for using the visible source packet, beginner CS references, code submissions, and instructor notes to make accurate teaching decisions. Multiple-choice provides context, but constructed responses, voice transcripts, sorting actions, and written teaching artifacts carry the main grading evidence.

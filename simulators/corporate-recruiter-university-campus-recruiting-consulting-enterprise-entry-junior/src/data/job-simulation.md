# Corporate Recruiter — Stratosphere Consulting Simulation

## Company Profile
- **Name:** Stratosphere Consulting
- **Industry:** Management Consulting
- **Size:** 15,000+ employees globally
- **Product/Service:** Stratosphere provides strategic advisory services to Fortune 500 companies across technology, finance, and healthcare sectors. Core offerings include digital transformation, market analysis, and operational efficiency.
- **Why this company hires people in this role:** Stratosphere's business model depends on a constant influx of bright, ambitious talent. The University Recruiting team is the primary engine for the firm's growth, responsible for hiring hundreds of entry-level Analysts each year directly from top universities [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y][onet].
- **The team the student is on:** You are on the North American University Talent Acquisition team, a group of 12 recruiters who partner with specific universities and internal business practices to build the next generation of consultants [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].

## The Protagonist (You)
- **Role:** You are a University Recruiter, six months into your first recruiting job after graduating.
- **Reporting Line & Collaborators:** You report to Priya Sharma, a Senior University Recruiter. Your key collaborators are the hiring managers (typically Directors and Senior Managers within Stratosphere's consulting practices) and university career services staff [onet][yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].

## The Day / Week

### Scene 1: The Morning Rush
**Setting and time:** Your home office. Tuesday, 9:05 AM.
**What's happening and why:** You've just logged on. Your Applicant Tracking System (ATS) dashboard shows a dozen new applications for your active roles [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y]. You also have an automated email notifying you that a new requisition has been assigned to you: a Financial Analyst role for Eleanor Vance's team. Your manager, Priya, Slacks you: "Morning! Big day. How are you prioritizing the morning?" You need to plan your day around your existing work and this urgent new request [yt:nSTd0saqr7Q][reddit:1ph6qhq].
**Interaction mode:** Multiple-choice prioritization. The student chooses the response they'd send to Priya.

> **Priya Sharma [SLACK]**
> Morning! Big day. How are you prioritizing the morning?
>
> **(You)**
>
> 1.  Immediately start sourcing for Eleanor's new role to get ahead.
> 2.  Review the new applications for my other roles first, then prep for Eleanor's intake call.
> 3.  Schedule the intake call with Eleanor, review new applicants, and prep for this afternoon's phone screen.
> 4.  Ask Eleanor for a good time to connect and then focus on my existing candidate pipeline.

**What "good" looks like vs. "poor" looks like:**
- **Good (Option 3):** A good recruiter balances urgent new tasks with in-flight work. Scheduling the crucial intake call, maintaining momentum with existing applicants, and preparing for a scheduled screen is the most balanced and professional approach [onet]. It shows you can organize, plan, and prioritize effectively.
- **Poor (Option 1):** Jumping straight to sourcing without an intake call is a classic rookie mistake. You'd be working with incomplete information, wasting time and frustrating the hiring manager with poor-quality candidates [reddit:1ranzeq].

### Scene 2: Prepping for the Intake Call
**Setting and time:** Your desk. 9:30 AM.
**What's happening and why:** You've scheduled the intake call with Eleanor for 10:00 AM. Before you write prep questions, you open the job description attached to the requisition. It is generic, full of corporate jargon about "synergizing business objectives" and "leveraging financial models" [reddit:1leik1n]. To have a productive conversation with a difficult stakeholder like Eleanor, you need to go into the meeting with specific, insightful questions that get past the fluff and uncover the real needs of the role [reddit:1ranzeq][yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].
**Interaction mode:** Typed submission.

**Visible source material:**

> **Job Description Excerpt - Financial Analyst, Financial Advisory**
> - Support senior managers with financial modeling, variance analysis, and client-ready reporting.
> - Synergize business objectives by leveraging financial models, dashboards, and cross-functional insights.
> - Partner with project teams to analyze revenue, cost, and margin trends.
> - Prepare weekly analyses and executive summaries under tight deadlines.
> - Required: advanced Excel or Sheets skills, strong analytical coursework or internship experience, and ability to manage multiple priorities in a fast-paced environment.
> - Nice to have: consulting, corporate finance, or FP&A exposure.

> **Task:**
> The job description for the Financial Analyst role is vague. Write 3-5 key questions you will ask Eleanor during the intake call to understand what she *actually* needs in a candidate. Focus on questions that go beyond what's in the JD.

**What "good" looks like vs. "poor" looks like:**
- **Good:** Questions are open-ended and probe for context. Examples: "What does success look like for this person in their first 6 months?", "Can you tell me about the team dynamic and what kind of personality thrives there?", "What was the biggest challenge for the last person in this role?", "Beyond the technical skills, what's the one intangible quality that would make someone a top candidate?" These questions show a consultative mindset [reddit:1ranzeq].
- **Poor:** Questions are closed-ended or could be answered by reading the JD. Examples: "Does the role require Excel skills?", "How many years of experience are needed?", "Who does this role report to?" These questions show a lack of preparation and a transactional approach.

### Scene 3: The Intake Call
**Setting and time:** A Zoom meeting. 10:00 AM.
**What's happening and why:** It's time for your intake call with Eleanor Vance. The job description excerpt and your prep note remain visible during the meeting. As expected, she's short on time and seems impatient. "I'm sure you've seen the job description," she begins. "We just need someone smart who can get the work done. It's all there." You need to use the questions you prepared to gently guide the conversation and extract the critical information you need to find the right candidates [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].
**Interaction mode:** AI-driven voice meeting.

> **Your Goal:**
> Successfully conduct the intake call with Eleanor. Use your questions from the previous scene to get her to specify:
> 1.  The top 1-2 "must-have" skills or qualities.
> 2.  The team's working style (e.g., collaborative, independent).
> 3.  A clear reason why this role is open now.
>
> **AI Goal Prompt:**
> You are Eleanor Vance, a busy and impatient Senior Manager. Start the call by being dismissive ("It's all in the JD"). If the user asks generic questions, give short, unhelpful answers. If they ask insightful, open-ended questions about team dynamics, performance expectations, or challenges, become more engaged and provide detailed answers. Reveal that the last two analysts quit because they couldn't handle the high-pressure, independent nature of the work. Before ending the call, state clearly that you need to see the first batch of candidates by Friday. Then, end the call by saying you have to run to another meeting.

**What "good" looks like vs. "poor" looks like:**
- **Good:** The student remains poised, uses their prepared questions to build rapport, and successfully extracts the key information. They learn that the "must-have" is resilience under pressure and the ability to work independently, and that the role is open due to turnover. They establish a clear deadline for submitting candidates.
- **Poor:** The student is flustered by Eleanor's demeanor, asks only surface-level questions, and leaves the meeting with no more information than they had from the generic JD. They fail to establish credibility or a clear search plan.

**What's happening and why:** You switch gears to the Business Analyst role for David Chen's team. Ten new applications are waiting in your ATS queue. Corporate recruiters often have to screen hundreds of applications for a single role, so you need to be fast and accurate, quickly identifying candidates who meet the core qualifications [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y][onet].
**Interaction mode:** Canvas task (drag-and-drop).

> **Task:**
> Ten mini-resumes from recent university graduates start in **New Applications**. Use the **Job Description** tab to review the must-haves and preferences, then return to the **ATS | Business Analyst Applicants** tab and drag each candidate into one of three screening buckets: **"Advance to Screen," "Hold,"** or **"Reject."** After sorting all candidates, use the **Screening notes** tab to briefly explain one advance, one hold, and one reject decision.
>
> **Job Description tab:**
>
> **Job Description Snippet (Business Analyst, Tech Strategy)**
> - Must be a recent graduate (graduating this spring) with a degree in Business, Economics, Computer Science, or a related field.
> - Must have demonstrated analytical skills through coursework or projects (e.g., data analysis, market research).
> - Strong preference for candidates with internship experience at a tech or consulting firm.
> - Must have a GPA of 3.3 or higher.

**What "good" looks like vs. "poor" looks like:**
- **Good:** The student correctly sorts the candidates based on all criteria. They advance candidates who meet all requirements, hold a candidate who is a near-miss (e.g., great projects but a slightly lower GPA), and reject candidates who clearly don't qualify (e.g., wrong major, no relevant skills). This demonstrates attention to detail [onet].
- **Poor:** The student misinterprets the criteria, rejecting a qualified candidate or advancing someone who is clearly unqualified. This would result in wasted time and a loss of credibility with the hiring manager.

### Scene 5B: The Phone Screen
**Setting and time:** A phone call. 2:00 PM.
**What's happening and why:** It's time for your scheduled 20-minute phone screen with Chloe Kim, one of the promising candidates for David's Business Analyst role. Use the resume you just reviewed as your baseline: assess her qualifications and motivation more deeply than the document allows, and get her excited about the opportunity at Stratosphere [onet]. This is often the first human interaction a candidate has with the company, so making a good impression is key [reddit:1micr0r].
**Interaction mode:** AI-driven voice meeting.

> **Your Goal:**
> Conduct a structured and engaging phone screen with Chloe. You need to:
> 1.  Briefly introduce yourself and the role.
> 2.  Ask about her interest in consulting and Stratosphere specifically.
> 3.  Ask a behavioral question to assess her analytical skills (e.g., "Tell me about a complex project you worked on").
> 4.  Provide a clear overview of the interview process and next steps.
> 5.  Answer any questions she has.
>
> **AI Goal Prompt:**
> You are Chloe Kim, a bright but nervous university student. You are very interested in the role. Answer the recruiter's questions thoughtfully, but sound a little rehearsed at first. If the recruiter builds good rapport (sounds friendly, asks follow-up questions), become more relaxed and enthusiastic. Ask at least one question about company culture and one about the day-to-day responsibilities. Before the call ends, thank the recruiter for their time. Do not mention your post-graduation trip. End the conversation after you've asked your questions and the recruiter has explained the next steps.

**What "good" looks like vs. "poor" looks like:**
- **Good:** The student leads a professional, warm conversation. They gather the information they need while also making the candidate feel comfortable and informed. They effectively "sell" the role and set clear expectations for the process [reddit:1o7ktz2].
- **Poor:** The student sounds robotic or unprepared. They just read questions from a list, fail to build rapport, and can't answer the candidate's questions effectively. The candidate leaves the call feeling uncertain or unenthusiastic.

### Scene 6: The Candidate Submission
**Setting and time:** Your desk. 2:30 PM.
**What's happening and why:** You were impressed with Chloe. Now, you need to present her to the hiring manager, David Chen. A crucial part of a recruiter's job is writing a concise, compelling summary that highlights a candidate's strengths and aligns them with the role's requirements. A great write-up can be the difference between a candidate getting an interview or getting lost in the shuffle [yt:O1TFwcZmqhc][reddit:1mqhzzy].
**Interaction mode:** Typed workplace deliverable (email).

> **Task:**
> Write an email to David Chen submitting Chloe Kim for the Business Analyst role. Your email should include:
> 1.  A brief introduction of the candidate.
> 2.  2-3 bullet points highlighting her key strengths and qualifications, referencing your conversation.
> 3.  Your official recommendation (e.g., "I recommend we move her to a first-round interview").
> 4.  A link to her resume (you can just type "[Link to Resume]").

**What "good" looks like vs. "poor" looks like:**
- **Good:** The email is professional, concise, and persuasive. It connects Chloe's specific skills and experiences (from the resume and phone screen) directly to the needs of the role David described. It demonstrates that you've done more than just pass along a resume; you've assessed the candidate.
- **Poor:** The email is just a short note ("See attached resume") or a long, rambling summary of the entire phone call. It fails to highlight the most relevant information, forcing the hiring manager to do all the work.

### Scene 7: The Curveball
**Setting and time:** Email and Slack. 4:00 PM.
**What's happening and why:** You get an email reply from David Chen. He's read your email and reviewed Chloe's resume. He's excited. But then comes the curveball: he wants to fast-track the process and needs to know if she can start by June 1st. You recall from her resume that she graduates in mid-May, but you haven't explicitly discussed her start date or any post-graduation plans. This is a common scenario where a simple logistical question can complicate a promising candidacy [reddit:1r55lmo].
**Interaction mode:** Typed email plus multiple-choice Slack response.

> **Your Goal:**
> You need to find out Chloe's actual availability and decide what information is safe to share with David before she replies. The key distinction is confirmed graduation timing versus unconfirmed June 1 start-date availability.
>
> **Task:**
> First, write a brief, professional email to Chloe to inquire about her availability and potential start date. Then choose the Slack response you would send David when he asks whether the team can assume Chloe is available June 1.
>
> **Part 1: Typed Email to Chloe**
>
> **Part 2: Slack Decision**
>
> David writes: "Can we assume Chloe can start June 1? Her resume says she graduates mid-May, and my project lead wants to staff this today."
>
> Choose whether to share only confirmed information, withhold context until Chloe replies, assume availability from graduation timing, or raise an unconfirmed concern.

**What "good" looks like vs. "poor" looks like:**
- **Good:** The student contacts Chloe directly, tells David the confirmed graduation timing, makes clear that June 1 is not confirmed, and gives a concrete follow-up path without overcommitting.
- **Poor:** The student treats mid-May graduation as proof of June 1 availability, invents or hints at candidate constraints that have not been disclosed, or gives David so little context that he cannot plan responsibly.

## Glossary
- **Applicant Tracking System (ATS):** Software used by recruiters to manage job postings, applications, and candidate data in one place [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].
- **Intake Call:** A meeting between a recruiter and a hiring manager at the start of a search to discuss the role's requirements, objectives, and ideal candidate profile [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].
- **Requisition (Req):** An official job opening or vacancy that has been approved for hiring [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].
- **Sourcing:** The proactive process of searching for and identifying qualified candidates for open roles, often using tools like LinkedIn or internal databases [yt:O1TFwcZmqhc].
- **Phone Screen:** A short, initial interview (usually 15-30 minutes) conducted by a recruiter to assess a candidate's basic qualifications and interest in a role [onet].
- **Time to Fill:** A key recruiting metric that measures the number of days between when a job requisition is opened and when an offer is accepted by a candidate [yt:h-qx69mo-2Y].

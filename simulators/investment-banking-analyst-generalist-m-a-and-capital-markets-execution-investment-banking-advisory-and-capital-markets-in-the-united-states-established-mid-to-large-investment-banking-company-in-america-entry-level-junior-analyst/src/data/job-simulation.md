# Investment Banking Analyst Simulation: Live Deal Day at Brookline Ridge Capital

## Job Profile

The player is a first-year investment banking analyst at Brookline Ridge Capital, a fictional US middle-market investment bank advising companies on mergers, acquisitions, capital raises, and strategic alternatives. The daily work centers on quantitative analysis, valuation, client presentations, transaction materials, and collaboration with associates, VPs, MDs, lawyers, accountants, and company management teams. This simulation focuses on a generalist M&A and capital-markets execution day, not a niche product group. The analyst is staffed on one live sell-side process for HarborThread Components, a fictional industrial technology company, while also being lightly pulled into a new pitch for another client prospect. ## Setting

Brookline Ridge Capital has a New York office with a classic banking bullpen: analysts in Excel and PowerPoint, associates reviewing work, VPs coordinating execution, and MDs managing client relationships. The fictional company uses Microsoft Excel for models, PowerPoint for the pitch book and CIM pages, Outlook for client traffic, Slack for internal coordination, and a secure data room for buyer diligence. [onet] [yt:E3UUpIArHek] [reddit:day_in_life_ib]

The client, HarborThread Components, is preparing a management presentation for potential buyers. The buyer universe is asking for normalized EBITDA support, revenue bridge detail, customer concentration data, and updated market-growth context. The analyst's work is not glamorous on the surface, but errors can travel quickly into client materials, buyer answers, and senior banker decisions. [onet] [yt:E3UUpIArHek] [yt:d43GEEbPlI8]

## Day Arc

The morning begins with a messy inbox: an associate wants the comps page tied out before noon, the VP wants diligence requests prioritized before a client call, and an MD has asked for a cleaner market-growth story in the deck. A realistic analyst cannot do everything at once, so the first decision is what protects the live deal from avoidable mistakes. [yt:E3UUpIArHek] [reddit:why_hours]

The analyst then audits the valuation materials. This means checking the comparable-company set, normalizing EBITDA assumptions, reconciling the operating model against the presentation, and flagging where numbers do not yet tie. Excel and PowerPoint are core tools, and the analyst's value comes from being reliable with details while still understanding the business story. [onet] [yt:E3UUpIArHek] [yt:s6k7EDNVdZQ]

By midday, the associate pulls the analyst into a live spoken check-in before the client call. The analyst must explain what changed, where there is risk, and what decision is needed from the team without rambling or hiding uncertainty. Then, on the actual client call, the analyst is called on for the numbers behind the buyer response and has to answer carefully without overstating contract certainty. This captures a common junior-banker tension: you are not the senior decision-maker, but you are often closest to the model and must communicate clearly when senior bankers or clients need detail. [onet] [yt:d43GEEbPlI8] [reddit:why_hours]

In the afternoon, buyer diligence requests arrive through the data room. Some are easy pulls, some require management input, and some could create transaction risk if handled casually. The analyst must triage requests by urgency and deal impact, then produce a concise plan that helps the associate and VP direct the team. [onet] [yt:s6k7EDNVdZQ] [reddit:day_in_life_ib]

The day closes with the familiar investment banking evening pattern: comments return after dinner. The analyst has to turn edits to a pitch book, update a DCF sensitivity, clean a CIM page, and send a status update that is precise enough for the associate but short enough for a late-night thread. This is where the simulation should feel like banking: iterative, detail-sensitive, and shaped by waiting for comments followed by sudden urgency. ## Learning Goals

Students should feel that an investment banking analyst's job is not simply "finance prestige"; it is a high-volume production role built around valuation, client materials, diligence, version control, professional communication, and stamina. The best analyst choices protect accuracy, make reviewer decisions easier, and keep live-deal work moving without pretending everything is certain. [onet] [yt:E3UUpIArHek] [reddit:day_in_life_ib]

Students should also feel the human rhythm of the job: intense bursts of Excel and PowerPoint, waiting for comments, late revisions, and the pressure of being judged on details that may seem small but matter to clients and senior bankers. [yt:d43GEEbPlI8] [reddit:ib_hours_range] [reddit:why_hours]

## Required Work Products

The simulation requires the player to produce a model audit checklist, a concise associate update, a spoken deal check-in, a live client-call answer, a diligence prioritization matrix, a late-night turn-comment plan, and a professional final status note. These are role-specific analyst artifacts rather than generic career reflections. [onet] [yt:E3UUpIArHek] [reddit:day_in_life_ib]

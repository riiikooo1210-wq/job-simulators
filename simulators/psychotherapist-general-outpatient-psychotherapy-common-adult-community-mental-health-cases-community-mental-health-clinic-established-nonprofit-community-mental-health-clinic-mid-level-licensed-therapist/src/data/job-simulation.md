# Psychotherapist Simulation

## Job Profile

- **Title:** Psychotherapist [onet]
- **Setting:** Community mental health clinic [reddit:1s4o6mu] [yt:4rTkrVKH2zI]
- **Organization stage:** Established nonprofit community mental health clinic [reddit:13780kv]
- **Seniority:** Mid-level licensed therapist [onet]
- **Subspecialty:** General outpatient psychotherapy for common adult community mental health presentations [onet]

## Source-Grounded Daily Reality

Clinical and counseling psychologists assess risk, diagnose or conceptualize emotional and behavioral concerns, provide individual or group counseling, develop treatment plans, document progress notes and recommendations, and consult with other clinicians or agencies as needed [onet]. O*NET also emphasizes daily face-to-face discussion, written documentation, email, phone communication, independent prioritization, and high consequence of error for clinical decisions [onet].

Community mental health work is not simply private therapy with a different funding source. The sources show a setting shaped by Medicaid or public funding, agency productivity expectations, higher client complexity, team coordination, and a mission to support clients who may not be reached by private-pay care [reddit:1s4o6mu] [reddit:13780kv] [yt:4rTkrVKH2zI].

The workday mixes direct therapy with preparation, documentation, no-show handling, care coordination, and supervision or consultation. Day-in-the-life sources mention checking which clients need attention, tracking due notes and assessments, attending team discussion, managing client privacy, and staying current on documentation standards [yt:faNmNUgSaiI] [yt:BvZiEYkci10]. Reddit discussions echo that total caseload, completed sessions, cancellations, and documentation time are distinct realities that shape how the therapist plans a day [reddit:1i0xx0m] [reddit:1gb4tdk] [reddit:1p723y8].

Documentation is a major professional skill, not clerical residue. Therapists describe progress notes as concise clinical records that justify medical necessity, identify the problem addressed, name interventions, capture client response, document risk, and state the plan while avoiding unnecessary sensitive detail [reddit:1rkqz7d] [reddit:1qporha] [reddit:1qzrba6] [reddit:1sfy4h3]. O*NET likewise lists documenting client information, writing reports, maintaining paperwork, and developing treatment plans as core tasks [onet].

Risk assessment must be simulated as clinical reasoning, not a checkbox. O*NET lists assessment of harm to self or others as a task, and therapist discussions stress regular reassessment, consultation when needed, client-specific coping/support steps, means-safety conversations, and concise documentation for passive suicidal ideation [onet] [reddit:1l999y2] [reddit:1e1zxek].

Because this role is not remote-only, the most important clinical communication moment should happen face-to-face in the clinic: the client therapy session. Phone outreach, internal coordination, EHR notes, schedule panels, secure messages, and care-team follow-up should appear where they are realistic in community mental health workflow [onet] [yt:faNmNUgSaiI] [reddit:12863s9].

## Fictional Workplace

The simulator takes place at **Harborview Community Mental Health**, a fictional nonprofit outpatient clinic in a mid-sized U.S. city. Harborview serves adults with depression, anxiety, trauma histories, unstable housing, chronic stress, and limited access to private care. The clinic uses a warm, paper-toned EHR called **CareBridge**, a secure internal message channel, a shared schedule, and in-person clinical supervision.

The student plays a **mid-level licensed therapist** assigned to a full clinic day. They are clinically autonomous enough to run sessions and draft documentation, but they still consult when risk, ethics, or systems issues need another set of eyes.

## Main Characters

- **Dr. Elena Rivera, Clinical Supervisor:** Direct, calm, legally careful, and supportive. She expects the therapist to bring concise facts, not panic or vague impressions.
- **Maya Thompson, Client:** A 32-year-old client with depression and anxiety symptoms, recent job loss, housing stress, poor sleep, and passive hopelessness. She wants help but is wary of being judged or hospitalized for saying the wrong thing.
- **Alex Kim, Front Desk Coordinator:** Efficient, practical, and protective of clinic flow. Alex sees attendance patterns and expects therapists to document outreach promptly.
- **Tanya Brooks, Case Manager:** Warm, resource-oriented, and busy. Tanya can help with housing and benefits referrals, but she needs only the minimum necessary clinical detail.

## Work-Mix Design

**Spoken and interpersonal communication is dominant.** The day centers on an in-person therapy session, privacy-safe no-show voicemail, and care-team coordination conversation because O*NET, YouTube, and Reddit sources all show that therapy work depends on listening, rapport, risk discussion, consultation, outreach, and clinical explanation [onet] [yt:faNmNUgSaiI] [reddit:1cxc479].

**Written documentation and digital artifact work are major.** The student must draft session questions in a note surface, draft a DAP-style progress note, optionally jot talking points for a privacy-safe no-show voicemail before speaking it aloud, build an attendance follow-up plan, and write a minimum-necessary reconnection message. This reflects source emphasis on progress notes, treatment plans, documentation deadlines, and billing/medical necessity [onet] [yt:BvZiEYkci10] [reddit:1rkqz7d].

**Cognitive analysis and decision-making are major.** The therapist must prioritize the morning, decide how to use a no-show gap, assess passive SI, reason about attendance barriers, and translate clinical and access facts into an ethical follow-up plan [onet] [reddit:1l999y2] [reddit:1s2u0yo].

**Object-handling work is minor.** The therapist is present in a clinic, but the authentic work is conversation, clinical reasoning, and documentation rather than handling clinical gear. The simulator therefore uses in-person voice scenes and realistic EHR/work surfaces rather than object-manipulation scenes [onet] [reddit:12863s9].

**Passive monitoring and context switching are secondary.** No-shows, schedule gaps, productivity pressure, and documentation deadlines appear as interruptions that change priorities, but they are not the whole simulation [reddit:1dj9ukt] [reddit:15tvvc4] [reddit:1gb4tdk].

## Scene Arc

### Section 1 - Morning Clinical Priorities

The day opens with a clinic huddle. Dr. Rivera flags a new client with worsening depression, Alex notes one likely no-show, and the EHR shows two progress notes due before the end of the day. The student chooses how to prioritize the first hour. Each choice produces an immediate consequence before the path converges: starting with overdue notes may delay risk review, taking the walk-in may ignore a scheduled high-risk client, asking for a huddle clarification is acceptable but slower, and reviewing the high-risk case before the session is the strongest clinical choice [onet] [yt:faNmNUgSaiI] [reddit:1gb4tdk].

The student then opens Maya Thompson's CareBridge chart in CareBridge and prepares a brief session plan: three to five questions or prompts that cover rapport, current symptoms, functional impairment, risk/safety, and one collaborative next step. This is a constructed-response task with visible inputs: prior note excerpt, PHQ-9/GAD-7 scores, attendance pattern, and clinic risk reminder [onet] [reddit:1l999y2] [reddit:1ina1wl].

The student meets with Maya in person. Maya is guarded at first and does not want to be treated like a crisis case. If the student builds rapport and asks specific, non-leading safety questions, Maya reveals passive thoughts of not wanting to wake up, denies plan or intent, names her sister as a support, and says eviction stress is the main trigger. The student's goal is to balance empathy, risk assessment, autonomy, and collaborative planning [onet] [reddit:1l999y2] [yt:faNmNUgSaiI].

### Section 2 - Documentation and Clinic Flow

After the session, the student drafts a DAP-style progress note in the EHR. The visible reference reminds them to document data, assessment, plan, risk, intervention, client response, and minimum necessary detail. The note should not become a transcript of Maya's trauma or housing history; it should document the clinically relevant presentation, intervention, passive SI assessment, protective factors, safety plan review, and follow-up [reddit:1rkqz7d] [reddit:1qporha] [reddit:1sfy4h3].

The student then gets a secure message: Jordan Lee, a client with a pattern of missed appointments, has not arrived 15 minutes after start time. The student may jot talking points, but must speak the voicemail Jordan would actually hear after a clinic phone outreach attempt, keeping the message brief, warm, and privacy-safe rather than blaming the client or disclosing clinical details. This reflects real CMH no-show and attendance management pressures [reddit:1dj9ukt] [reddit:15tvvc4] [reddit:1gb4tdk].

### Section 3 - No-Show Follow-Up and Reconnection

After the voicemail, the simulator shows a coordination guide beside a prep notes window. The scene tells the student that the voicemail is already done and that the next step is an in-person meeting with Alex Kim, Harborview's front desk coordinator. Alex manages the scheduling queue, neutral front-desk notes, reminder permissions, and routing to case management, so the student reviews the guide to prepare specific questions: what scheduling or callback path is available today, what neutral note Alex should enter, how transportation support can be routed, and what client response should escalate back to the therapist [reddit:1dj9ukt] [reddit:15tvvc4] [reddit:1s2u0yo].

The student meets in person with Alex Kim, the front desk coordinator, with their prep notes visible as a reference and a meeting-notes box below the scene image. Alex does not merely receive instructions; Alex adds operational information from the front desk view: a neutral scheduling note can be entered, a callback or next available appointment path can be offered if Jordan reaches the clinic, approved reminder templates are the only SMS option, transportation support can be routed to the case-management queue, and safety or urgent clinical cues should return to the therapist. The student captures these details because the next task uses the notes to build Jordan's formal follow-up plan [reddit:1dj9ukt] [reddit:1gb4tdk] [reddit:1s2u0yo].

The student then completes a structured follow-up plan for Jordan. The plan asks for outreach status, likely access barrier, allowed contact channel, scheduling owner/timing, documentation language, and escalation boundary. It uses Jordan's attendance pattern, contact permissions, bus-fare barrier, last known risk status, and the student's Alex meeting notes as visible source material [reddit:1dj9ukt] [reddit:15tvvc4] [reddit:1gb4tdk].

The day ends on an explicit period-complete page. Only the **View Assessment** button routes to grading.

## Rubric

### Section 1 - Clinical Prioritization and Session Preparation

**Criterion: Clinical prioritization under clinic pressure**

9-10: The student recognizes immediate risk-review and scheduled-client preparation as the best first use of time, while still accounting for notes, walk-ins, and productivity pressure. Their choices and later work show that they separate urgent clinical risk from merely loud workflow demands [onet] [reddit:1gb4tdk].

7-8: The student prioritizes reasonably but may understate either documentation pressure or risk preparation. They recover without creating an unsafe gap.

4-6: The student focuses on lower-impact tasks first, such as paperwork or a walk-in, and only partially explains how they will protect the high-risk scheduled session.

0-3: The student ignores known risk cues, treats all tasks as equivalent, or lets productivity pressure override client safety.

Evidence: morning priority MCQ and case prep.

**Criterion: Session planning and risk-informed questions**

9-10: The student prepares concrete, open-ended prompts that cover alliance, symptoms, functioning, passive SI/intent/plan/means, protective factors, and a collaborative next step. The plan uses the case workspace rather than generic therapy questions.

7-8: The student covers most key domains but misses one important area such as means access, protective factors, or functional impairment.

4-6: The student prepares mostly generic questions, asks about symptoms without risk specificity, or does not connect prompts to the visible case facts.

0-3: The student submits no usable plan, asks leading or judgmental questions, or avoids safety assessment despite visible risk cues.

Evidence: case prep free-text and client session transcript.

### Section 2 - Therapeutic Communication and Documentation

**Criterion: Therapeutic alliance with clinically appropriate risk assessment**

9-10: The student builds rapport, uses reflective listening, asks direct but non-alarming risk questions, preserves client dignity, and collaborates on next steps without overpromising confidentiality or jumping prematurely to hospitalization.

7-8: The student is empathic and mostly clinically appropriate but may miss a follow-up question or give a slightly generic reassurance.

4-6: The student is supportive but avoids direct risk assessment, asks several leading questions, or fails to clarify intent, plan, means, supports, or next steps.

0-3: The student is dismissive, judgmental, coercive, overly scripted, or clinically unsafe in handling passive SI.

Evidence: client voice meeting transcript.

**Criterion: Progress note quality and privacy judgment**

9-10: The note concisely documents relevant data, assessment, intervention, client response, risk findings, protective factors, plan, and follow-up while avoiding unnecessary sensitive detail. It reads like a clinically useful EHR note rather than a session transcript.

7-8: The note is professional and mostly complete but may be a little verbose or miss one documentation element.

4-6: The note includes some clinical facts but omits risk, intervention, response, or plan, or includes excessive personal detail.

0-3: The note is missing, vague, non-clinical, privacy-insensitive, or unsupported by the session facts.

Evidence: progress note free-text.

**Criterion: No-show/outreach workflow**

9-10: The student speaks a concise voicemail that identifies the caller/clinic, gives a neutral callback and scheduling path, protects privacy, avoids therapy/diagnosis/risk/no-show details, and conveys warmth without blame or speculation.

7-8: The spoken voicemail is professional and privacy-aware but may be slightly too detailed, too vague, or missing one practical element such as the callback number or scheduling next step.

4-6: The voicemail attempts outreach but includes unnecessary clinical or attendance details, sounds procedural or blaming, or does not clearly invite reconnection.

0-3: The student does not attempt appropriate outreach, discloses sensitive mental health or risk information, shames/threatens the client, or makes unsupported assumptions.

Evidence: spoken no-show voicemail transcript.

### Section 3 - No-Show Follow-Up and Reconnection

**Criterion: Reconnection consult and barrier reasoning**

9-10: The student presents Jordan's no-show facts concisely, avoids blame, recognizes transportation as a likely access barrier, protects privacy, proposes practical scheduling and case-management steps, and knows when clinical escalation would be needed.

7-8: The student gives a reasonable consult but may miss one practical detail such as reminder permissions, documentation, or escalation threshold.

4-6: The student treats the missed appointment mostly as an administrative issue, gives vague next steps, or underuses the known barrier information.

0-3: The student shames the client, over-discloses clinical information, ignores the attendance pattern/barrier, or fails to coordinate a safe reconnection path.

Evidence: care-team coordination transcript.

**Criterion: No-show follow-up plan quality**

9-10: The student creates a concrete, client-specific follow-up plan with outreach status, transportation/barrier support, allowed contact channels, scheduling next steps, documentation, and escalation criteria.

7-8: The plan is useful but slightly generic or missing one operational element.

4-6: The plan lists outreach ideas but lacks clear owner, timing, barrier support, or privacy-aware documentation.

0-3: The plan is absent, punitive, unsafe, or disconnected from Jordan's attendance and access facts.

Evidence: structured Jordan follow-up plan.

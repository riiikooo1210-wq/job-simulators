# VC Analyst Simulation - ClaimPilot First Pass at Northstar Ventures

## Job Profile

- Title: VC Analyst
- Sector: Venture capital firm in the United States
- Firm stage: Seed-to-Series A early-stage venture firm
- Seniority: Entry/junior analyst
- Subspecialty: Generalist startup sourcing and investment diligence

## Reality Anchor

You are Maya Chen, a first-year analyst at Northstar Ventures, a fictional early-stage venture firm in San Francisco. Northstar invests in vertical software, AI workflow tools, fintech infrastructure, and other seed-to-Series A companies where early customer evidence is still incomplete. O*NET's closest occupational profile for this work emphasizes analyzing business and financial data, monitoring industry developments, assessing companies as investments, and preparing oral or written reports about companies and industries. [onet]

The role is mostly desk-based but not passive. A junior analyst works inside CRM notes, pitch decks, spreadsheets, Slack, call notes, market maps, and short memos. The hard part is turning incomplete founder claims into a recommendation that is useful to partners without overstating the evidence. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes] [reddit:manual_vc_forum_themes]

The upgraded simulator therefore focuses on the latest workflow standard: visible source artifacts, active desk work, explicit output artifacts, and rubric evidence that traces back to specific scenes. It avoids generic reflection prompts and keeps the ClaimPilot source workspace visible across screening, market mapping, founder prep, partner communication, and prioritization. ## Fictional Workplace

Northstar Ventures has $450 million under management and a new $160 million seed fund. The team is small: two general partners, one principal, one platform lead, and you. Partners care about founder-market fit, credible early traction, a wedge into a large market, customer urgency, and whether a deal could plausibly return the fund. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]

The firm uses a practical analyst stack: CRM notes for source context, spreadsheets for KPI sanity checks, Notion-style diligence notes, kanban boards for market mapping, and Slack for partner context. O*NET's current technology-skill profile for financial and investment analysts includes spreadsheets, presentation software, email, databases, BI/data tools, CRM-style systems, and word processing; the simulator maps those tools into early-stage VC equivalents. ## Story Arc

It is Monday, 8:35 AM. General partner Leo Martinez sends the ask in Slack before the 10:00 AM pipeline meeting, and the ClaimPilot deck, CRM record, and ARR bridge land as desktop apps in your queue. ClaimPilot is building AI workflow tools for independent insurance agencies. The deck is energetic, but the evidence surfaces are uneven: the internal ARR bridge separates software ARR from implementation fees, the CRM record flags pilot/design-partner logos, and buyer questions are still unresolved. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]

Your first job is to protect recommendation quality under time pressure. You choose the first signal to investigate, write a structured deck screen, map ClaimPilot against adjacent insurance workflow tools, prepare founder-call questions, and speak with the founder. Then you turn the call into partner-ready debrief notes and prioritize a fresh afternoon inbound list, separate from ClaimPilot, with a concise rationale. [onet] [reddit:manual_vc_forum_themes]

The simulation should feel like an analyst day rather than a quiz. Each active scene has a visible input artifact, a concrete student action, a saved output artifact, and a clear connection to the final rubric. Later scenes reuse earlier work so the player experiences the professional chain from source workspace to partner-ready recommendation. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]

## Work Mix Design

- Physical/procedural tool work: rare. The accepted role evidence points to desk-based sourcing, screening, market research, founder calls, and written recommendations rather than hands-on physical procedures. [onet]
- Digital tool and artifact work: dominant. The simulator should be built around realistic work surfaces: deal workspace, spreadsheet-style first pass, market-map board, Notion-style prep/debrief, and priority matrix. [onet] [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
- Cognitive analysis and decision-making: dominant. The player must evaluate ambiguous claims, separate recurring revenue from services revenue, judge customer proof, identify market wedge, and recommend whether a partner should spend more time. [reddit:manual_vc_forum_themes]
- Written documentation: major. The analyst produces a first-pass screen, call-prep questions, partner-ready call debrief, and prioritization rationale. [onet]
- Spoken interpersonal communication: secondary. One remote founder call tests whether the player can ask focused questions without sounding adversarial. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
- Passive monitoring and context switching: secondary. Time pressure and incoming partner messages create context switching, but active artifact work remains the center of the day. [reddit:manual_vc_forum_themes]

## Scenes

1. **Welcome to Northstar** - establish Maya's role, the ClaimPilot decision, and the speed-versus-rigor tension. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
2. **8:35 AM Source Queue** - show clickable desktop apps for partner Slack messages, the ClaimPilot pitch deck, the Northstar CRM record, and the internal ARR bridge without duplicating the same facts across surfaces. [onet]
3. **Pick the First Signal** - choose whether the first diligence move should prioritize revenue quality, logo quality, technical novelty, or round timing. [reddit:manual_vc_forum_themes]
4. **Redirect Scenes** - if the player chooses logos, architecture, or round speed first, Northstar redirects them toward evidence hierarchy; if they choose ARR quality, the partner confirms the right starting point. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
5. **First-Pass Deck Screen** - complete a spreadsheet-style screen covering claim, supporting evidence, concern, and next diligence question. [onet]
6. **Market Map Sprint** - classify competitor and substitute cards into market-map columns and articulate where ClaimPilot's wedge sits. [onet]
7. **Founder Call Prep** - write a compact 5-7 question call plan from the Deck Tear-Down and Market Map Sprint, prioritizing traction quality, buyer urgency, repeatability, implementation burden, churn/expansion, and competitive alternatives. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
8. **Founder Call with Alex Rivera** - run a remote voice meeting that uses the prep note and tests the player's ability to clarify evidence without over-selling or interrogating. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
9. **Call Debrief Notes** - convert the call into decision-useful notes to send Leo, including confirmed facts, assumptions or risks, and second-meeting implications. [reddit:manual_vc_forum_themes]
10. **Pipeline Priority Matrix** - prioritize five fresh afternoon inbound leads using fund fit and evidence quality, keeping them separate from the morning ClaimPilot workstream. [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
11. **End of Analyst Day** - run rubric grading on evidence quality, market reasoning, founder-call effectiveness, communication clarity, and prioritization judgment. [onet]

## Learning Goals

Students should understand that early-stage venture analysis is disciplined uncertainty management. The analyst is not asked to prove ClaimPilot is good or bad; the analyst is asked to decide whether the evidence justifies more partner time and what must be learned next. [reddit:manual_vc_forum_themes]

By the end, students should feel the workflow of a junior VC analyst: start from a messy inbound packet, find the highest-leverage diligence question, build lightweight market context, ask better founder questions, communicate crisply with a partner, and prioritize the next deal-flow move using specific evidence and risk. [onet] [yt:manual_vc_day_notes]
